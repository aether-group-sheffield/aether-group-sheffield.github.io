Research

What the group works on

AETHER's research sits at the meeting point of engineering and healthcare. Three themes organise our work, and most projects draw on more than one of them.

Theme 01

Artificial Intelligence

Data-driven methods for understanding health data and supporting clinical decision-making — developed responsibly, with clinicians in the loop.

Healthcare generates rich, complex data, and making sense of it safely is an engineering problem as much as a computational one. The group develops and applies machine learning methods to clinical and physiological data, always with attention to how a model's outputs will actually be used — and trusted — in practice.

Areas of interest include

  • Machine learning for clinical and physiological signal analysis
  • Decision-support tools that keep clinicians in control
  • Interpretable and trustworthy models for healthcare settings
  • Responsible handling of sensitive health data
Theme 02

Medical Devices

Instrumentation and device engineering for monitoring, diagnosis, and treatment — designed for the people who will use them.

From sensing and electronics through to usability and evaluation, the group works on the full journey of a medical device. Our electrical and electronic engineering base gives us the tools to prototype quickly, while our healthcare partnerships keep development grounded in clinical reality.

Areas of interest include

  • Sensing and instrumentation for physiological measurement
  • Wearable and point-of-care technologies
  • Device prototyping, evaluation, and usability
  • Pathways from prototype to clinical translation
Theme 03

Automatic Control

Systems and control engineering applied to physiology and therapy — modelling how the body behaves and building technology that responds to it.

Control engineering offers a powerful language for healthcare: physiological systems can be modelled, monitored, and — in some cases — regulated. The group applies systems modelling and feedback control to problems where a technology must adapt to a patient in real time.

Areas of interest include

  • Modelling and identification of physiological systems
  • Closed-loop and feedback approaches to therapy and assistance
  • Signal processing for monitoring and estimation
  • Safety and robustness in patient-facing control systems
